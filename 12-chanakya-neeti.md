# Chanakya Neeti — Chanakya (Ancient India)

## Overview

Written by Chanakya (Kautilya, also known as Vishnu Gupta), the political strategist who unified ancient India and founded the Mauryan Empire in 4th century BCE. Chanakya Neeti is a collection of aphorisms on statecraft, wealth, ethics, and human nature. Older than Machiavelli, eerily similar in some ways — but with a stronger moral dimension.

Not a book in the modern sense — a set of practical maxims derived from observation of human nature and political reality.

## Key Themes

### On Self-Interest

Chanakya is ruthlessly realistic about human motivation.

> The world exists for the enjoyment of the king (leader). All others exist for the enjoyment of the king.

Reality check: people serve their own interests first. Structure incentives accordingly. This is both cynical and practical.

### On Testing Character

Test a person's character before trusting them:

- Test gold by fire
- Test a woman's loyalty by giving her money
- Test a servant by giving them a difficult task
- Test a relative by sharing wealth with them

People reveal themselves when something is at stake.

### On Rulers and Power

A king (leader) must be:

- Strong enough to protect — weak kings invite enemies
- Just, but not so just that they are exploited
- Surrounded by competent advisors, not sycophants
- Aware that the people are the true source of power

> Just as a tree is known by its fruit, a king is known by his treasury.

### On Wealth

- Wealth acquired easily is spent easily
- Wealth preserved should be used strategically
- Greed destroys wealth — generosity protects it
- A merchant who fears loss cannot earn great profit

### On Relationships

- A friend in need is a friend indeed — test friendship by creating need
- Flattery is the enemy of truth
- The company you keep shapes your character
- A wife is known by how her husband behaves in public and private

### On Wisdom and Conduct

- Speak only if your words are more valuable than silence
- The wise man learns from his enemies
- Patience is the greatest strength
- Pride before a fall — the arrogant are always brought low

## Key Maxims

> A man is known by his deeds — not by his family.
> A bird does not fly alone — it is known by its company.
> The king who respects his elders will never be without followers.
> The clever man learns from the mistakes of others.
> He who is too busy to look after his health will soon be too busy to do anything else.
> A father's gift to his children is education; a teacher's gift is character.
> A woman without a husband is like a house without a roof.
> The poison of a snake is in its tail — the poison of a bad man is in his wealth.

## For Founders

- Chanakya on incentives: people optimize for what they're measured on — design measurement systems carefully
- On advisors: surround yourself with truth-tellers, not sycophants
- On power: legitimacy comes from delivering value to stakeholders, not just extracting it
- On patience: the tortoise wins the long race

## One-Line Takeaway

> Understand human nature as it is, not as you wish it to be — then navigate with wisdom and restraint.
