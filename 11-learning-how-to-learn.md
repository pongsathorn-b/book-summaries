# Learning How to Learn — Barbara Oakley

## Overview

Barbara Oakley was a language major who struggled with math — then discovered she's not "bad at math," she'd just never learned how to learn. She reverse-engineered learning itself and went on to co-create one of the world's most popular online courses (Learning How to Learn on Coursera, taken by millions).

## Core Concepts

### Focused Mode vs Diffuse Mode

Two thinking modes:

**Focused Mode:** Direct, analytical, linear thinking. Like a focused laser beam. What you use when studying a specific concept, doing math, reading a textbook.

**Diffuse Mode:** Relaxed, big-picture thinking. Like a floodlight. This is when your brain makes connections, processes what you've learned, and has insights. Active during walks, showers, sleep.

The key insight: **You need both modes.** Focused to learn the material, diffuse to consolidate and connect it. Forcing focused mode 24/7 is counterproductive.

### Chunking

A chunk is a mental package of information that fits together — a compressed unit of meaning.

- Novices see bits; experts see chunks
- Chunking is how experts process information so efficiently
- To build chunks: focused study, understanding the core idea, practice with retrieval
- Context (when and how to use a chunk) is as important as the chunk itself

### The Illusion of Knowing

Rereading and highlighting create the illusion of mastery — you recognize the material, so it feels like you know it. But recognition is not recall. Real learning happens when you struggle to retrieve information without looking at it.

**Test yourself — that's when real learning happens.**

### Spaced Repetition

Cramming feels productive but doesn't stick. Better: spread study over time with increasing intervals.

- Day 1: learn it
- Day 3: review it
- Day 7: review it
- Day 14: review it

Each review strengthens the neural pathway. Sleep between sessions matters — the brain consolidates during sleep.

### Procrastination and Zombie Mode

Procrastination activates the pain centers of the brain — so we avoid the task. Willpower alone doesn't fix this. The solution: **make the task small enough that your zombie mode (habit) can handle it without pain.**

- 2-minute rule: if it takes less than 2 minutes, just do it now
- The zombie mode builds over time — consistency creates automaticity
- Planning is important: write tomorrow's specific plan tonight

### Memory Techniques

**Working memory** holds ~4 items at a time. **Long-term memory** is vast but requires patterns to store.

- **Spaced repetition** (see above)
- **Memory palaces**: visualize placing memories in a familiar location
- **Interleaving**: mix different topics during study rather than blocking them
- **Transfer**: learning one subject helps you learn another — even when they seem unrelated

### The Value of Mistakes

Making mistakes during practice is a feature, not a bug. Mistakes activate the diffuse mode and help identify gaps. The "desirable difficulty" principle: harder practice leads to better long-term retention.

## For Founders

- Building a startup is constant learning under uncertainty — understand your own learning style and optimize for it
- The focused/diffuse switch is essentially: work on the problem (focused) then sleep on it (diffuse) — often you wake up with the solution
- Interleaving helps with strategy: don't just study one thing at a time, cross-reference

## One-Line Takeaway

> Learning is a skill — switch between focused and diffuse thinking, test yourself constantly, and spread practice over time.
