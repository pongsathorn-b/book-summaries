# Nonviolent Communication — Marshall Rosenberg

> \"When we communicate from the belief that everyone's needs matter — and that there's always a way to meet everyone's needs — violence disappears from our hearts and from our world.\"

## Overview

Clinical psychologist [[Marshall Rosenberg]] developed Nonviolent Communication (NVC) after a decade of working with communities torn apart by violence — from American inner cities to war zones in Rwanda and the Middle East. What he discovered was that beneath every conflict, every act of violence, every fractured relationship, lies a fundamental failure of communication: people have forgotten how to articulate their needs without judgment and how to hear another's needs without defense. NVC offers a complete communication methodology — with a specific four-step process — that replaces moralistic judgments, punishment, and coercion with honest expression rooted in universal human needs, and empathetic listening that connects rather than divides. Far more than a conflict resolution technique, NVC is a philosophy of human interaction that Rosenbergs believes can transform every relationship, organization, and society.

## Core Concepts

### The Four Components of NVC
NVC structures every communication into four elements, which can be used for both expressing yourself and empathetically receiving others:

**1. Observations (without evaluation)** — Describe what is happening in factual, non-judgmental language. The key is to separate observation from evaluation — to notice what actually happened without layering moral judgment onto it. \"He said 'That's stupid'\" is an observation. \"He is an idiot\" is an evaluation masquerading as an observation.

**2. Feelings (without thoughts)** — Identify and express what you are feeling — not what you think about the other person, but your actual emotional state. Rosenberg provides extensive lists of feelings vocabulary organized by whether your needs are being met (fulfilled, excited, relieved) or unmet (frustrated, sad, anxious). \"I feel hurt\" is a feeling; \"I feel like you're attacking me\" is a thought.

**3. Needs (universal human needs, not strategies)** — Connect the feeling to the underlying human need or value that is being met or unmet. Needs are universal — everyone shares them: safety, autonomy, connection, meaning, play, growth. What differs are the strategies for meeting those needs. \"I need respect\" is more precise than \"I need you to stop talking that way\" because it names the underlying need rather than one particular strategy.

**4. Requests (specific, doable, positive actions)** — Make a clear, specific request for what you would like — phrased as positive action (what you want to happen, not what you want to stop happening) rather than a demand. A good request is: concrete, doable within the other's capacity, and something the other can say yes or no to freely.

### The Two Parts of NVC
NVC has two dimensions:

**Expressing Honestly** — Using the four components (Observation → Feeling → Need → Request) to express your own experience without blame or demand. \"I noticed dishes left in the sink again. I feel frustrated because my need for a clean shared space isn't met. Would you be willing to put your dishes in the dishwasher before bed?\" replaces the habitual: \"You're so inconsiderate. You never clean up after yourself.\"

**Receiving Empathically** — Listening for the same four elements in the other person's communication — especially hearing the feelings and needs beneath what might sound like blame, criticism, or attack. When someone says \"You're selfish,\" they are probably expressing an unmet need for consideration or connection, likely through a blocked feeling. Empathic listening means hearing that need without taking the words literally.

### The Spectrum of Communication
Rosenberg maps all human communication along a spectrum from coercive to empathetic:

- **Moralistic judgments** — evaluating others as good or bad based on whether they meet our needs
- **Blame, insults, put-downs, labels** — language that creates disconnection and triggers defensive reactions
- **Polarized right/wrong thinking** — seeing the world as requiring punishment for those who are \"wrong\"
- **NVC consciousness** — focusing on universal human needs and finding strategies to meet everyone's needs

### Life-Alienating vs. Life-Enriching Communication
Rosenberg distinguishes between communication that increases violence and disconnection (\"life-alienating\") and communication that enriches life and connection (\"life-enriching\"). Life-alienating communication is characterized by moralistic judgments, denial of responsibility (\"I have to do this\"), and demands (\"you must\"). Life-enriching communication takes responsibility for feelings (acknowledging that our feelings come from our own needs, not others' behavior), uses language that presumes mutually compatible needs, and makes requests rather than demands.

### NVC and Self-Empathy
A critical (and often overlooked) component of NVC is self-empathy — applying the four-step process to your own inner dialogue. When you are in pain, angry, or triggered, NVC teaches you to: observe what happened, identify the feeling, connect it to the unmet need, and make a request of yourself or life. Self-empathy prevents you from needing others to meet all your needs and gives you access to compassion even in solitude.

## Key Lessons

1. **Observations and evaluations are not the same thing — and confusing them destroys communication** — \"You always ignore me\" triggers defensiveness; \"You didn't respond to my last three texts\" creates space for dialogue
2. **Your feelings come from your needs, not from others' behavior** — taking responsibility for your feelings (\"I feel lonely because I need connection\") is more empowering than making others responsible for how you feel
3. **Behind every \"should,\" \"have to,\" and \"must\" is an unmet need** — \"I should lose weight\" is really \"I have a need for health and vitality\"; reconnecting with the underlying need clarifies choices
4. **Requests and demands look identical until the other person says no** — a request invites cooperation; a demand creates resentment if compliance is required; the difference is in how you react to \"no\"
5. **Empathy is listening for the feeling and need beneath the words and behavior** — when someone attacks, they need something desperately; empathic listening hears the need behind the attack
6. **NVC is not about getting what you want — it's about finding strategies that meet everyone's needs** — this shifts the goal from winning to collaborating
7. **Gratitude is best expressed as an NVC observation** — \"Thank you for picking up the kids today\" followed by the need it met (\"I was exhausted and needed support\") is more meaningful than generic thanks
8. **Saying \"no\" without guilt and hearing \"no\" without attack** — NVC helps you honor your own boundaries while remaining open to understanding the other's needs
9. **Self-empathy is the foundation of compassionate relationships** — you cannot give others the empathy you haven't first given yourself
10. **NVC requires practice because the habit of judgment runs deep** — the brain defaults to evaluation because it's faster; NVC is a deliberate rewiring that takes conscious daily practice

## Practical Applications

- **Practice the four-step NVC process in low-stakes situations first** — use it when asking someone to pass the salt before applying it to relationship conflicts
- **Replace \"you\" statements with observations** — train yourself to notice specific, evaluative-free behaviors before adding any feeling or judgment
- **Build a feelings vocabulary list** — many people can only name a few emotions; expand your emotional vocabulary to distinguish between nuanced states
- **Listen for needs, not just words** — when a partner, colleague, or child is upset, instead of reacting to their words, ask yourself: \"What need is this person trying to get met?\"
- **Make \"positive\" requests** — phrase what you want to happen rather than what you want to stop: \"Would you be willing to tell me when you'll be home?\" instead of \"Don't just disappear without telling me\"
- **Practice self-empathy when triggered** — pause, breathe, and apply the NVC process to yourself: What happened? What am I feeling? What need of mine is unmet? What can I request of myself right now?
- **Express appreciation using all three NVC components** — describe the specific action, the feeling it generated, and the need it met: \"When you texted me during your commute — I felt cared for and less anxious — because I have a need for reassurance during hard moments\"
- **Use NVC to set boundaries without guilt** — \"I hear that you want X, and my answer is no. I want to honor my need for rest, and I also value our relationship — they are not the same thing.\"

## Controversy/Criticism

Critics argue that NVC can feel robotic or formulaic when applied rigidly, and that the four-step process may be impractical in high-stress situations where people are dysregulated. Some psychologists note that the emphasis on universal needs may oversimplify the complexity of conflicting values and that NVC's refusal to engage with judgment can feel evasive in situations that genuinely require moral accountability (abuse, betrayal, systemic injustice). Others worry that the framework can be used manipulatively — to reframe coercive behavior as \"meeting everyone's needs\" or to pressure victims into empathizing with their abusers. Finally, some people find the language of NVC (especially its focus on \"needs\" and \"feelings\") culturally incompatible with communication styles that value indirectness, hierarchy, or emotional restraint.

## One-Line Takeaway

Nonviolent Communication transforms conflict by shifting the question from \"who is right and who is wrong\" to \"what unmet needs are driving this behavior, and how can we find strategies that serve us both.\"
