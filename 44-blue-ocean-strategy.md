# Blue Ocean Strategy — W. Chan Kim & Renée Mauborgne

> "Compete in overcrowded industries, and you'll drown in commoditization. Create blue oceans, and the rules of competition become irrelevant."

## Overview

[[W. Chan Kim]] and [[Renée Mauborgne]], professors at INSEAD, challenge the fundamental assumption of business strategy: that competing in crowded, red ocean markets is inevitable. Their research across 150+ strategic moves over 100 years found that companies that created new market space — which they call *blue oceans* — outperformed competitors who fought tooth-and-nail in established markets (*red oceans*). The key is *value innovation*: instead of trying to outcompete rivals on existing factors, you simultaneously pursue differentiation *and* low cost by eliminating, reducing, raising, and creating factors the industry has never offered. This is the Strategy Canvas and the Four Actions Framework.

## Core Concepts

### 1. Red Ocean vs. Blue Ocean Markets
- **Red oceans** are known market spaces where industry boundaries are defined and competition is fierce. Products become commodities. Margins compress. Growth is zero-sum.
- **Blue oceans** are uncontested market spaces where demand is created rather than fought over. Competition is irrelevant because the rules of the game haven't been defined yet.

Most value-creating innovations come from blue oceans, not from red ocean competition.

### 2. Value Innovation
Value innovation occurs when a company's actions simultaneously affect its ability to differentiate *and* its cost structure. Traditional competitive strategy forces a trade-off: differentiate (higher cost) or cut costs (lower differentiation). Value innovation eliminates the trade-off by reconceptualizing what the industry actually offers.

### 3. The Strategy Canvas
A two-axis framework: horizontal axis shows the factors the industry invests in and competes on; vertical axis shows the level of offering buyers receive on each factor. A blue ocean strategy is visualized as shifting the curve — eliminating factors the industry takes for granted, reducing others below the industry standard, raising others above standard, and creating entirely new factors no competitor has offered.

### 4. The Four Actions Framework
To reconstruct buyer value, ask four questions:
- **Eliminate**: Which factors that the industry takes for granted should be eliminated entirely?
- **Reduce**: Which factors should be reduced well below the industry standard?
- **Raise**: Which factors should be raised well above the industry standard?
- **Create**: Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?

The Four Actions framework is how you actually build the Strategy Canvas.

### 5. The Six Paths to Blue Ocean
Rather than brainstorming abstractly, the authors identified six concrete paths to blue ocean creation:
1. **Across alternative industries** (what else do people use instead?)
2. **Across strategic groups** (within your industry, which group do you belong to and what would happen at a different tier?)
3. **Across buyer groups** (who is actually making the buying decision, and could you serve a different buyer?)
4. **Complementary product and service offerings** (what does the product lack that would complete the customer's job-to-be-done?)
5. **Functional-emotional orientation** (could you shift the basis of competition from functional to emotional or vice versa?)
6. **Time** (what would happen if you anticipated shifts in industry events that competitors aren't preparing for?)

### 6. Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create Grid
A structured tool used to force trade-off decisions. Teams must fill out the grid collaboratively to identify which factors to eliminate, which to reduce, which to raise, and which to create. The grid prevents the trap of incremental improvement within existing competitive boundaries.

## Key Lessons

1. **The goal is not to beat the competition — it's to make the competition irrelevant.** If you're thinking about competitors, you're already trapped in the red ocean.
2. **Value innovation requires simultaneously lowering costs and increasing differentiation.** If you're only doing one, you're not creating a blue ocean.
3. **Blue oceans are not limited to tech or high-growth industries.** Cirque du Soleil, Ford Model T, and the Nasdaq all created blue oceans in seemingly mature, established industries.
4. **Use the Six Paths as a structural forcing mechanism** — they're more reliable than open-ended brainstorming for finding blue ocean opportunities.
5. **The strategy canvas forces you to visualize the industry's existing rules** — you can't break rules you don't know you're following.
6. **The Four Actions framework is a decision filter** — any factor that doesn't fall clearly into one of the four actions is suspect. If you can't categorize it, it probably won't create a blue ocean.
7. **Blue ocean strategy is not just for startups.** Large companies like IBM, Ford, and Samsung have used it to create new growth platforms.
8. **Fair process is essential for execution.** People execute what they understand and accept. Without fair process — engagement, explanation, and clarity — even the best strategy fails.
9. **The tipping point headwind** — in any market, a small number of factors accounts for the majority of your results. Focus on those first.
10. **Blue oceans have limits.** Once you've created one, competitors will try to imitate. The goal is to build a blue ocean *and* make it hard to copy through brand, network effects, or regulatory moats.

## Practical Applications

- **Draw your industry's current strategy canvas.** What are the factors everyone competes on? What's the level of each? You'll be surprised how similar all competitors look.
- **Apply the Six Paths framework** to your business: Could you serve a different buyer group? Could you eliminate the functional/emotional trade-off? What would your industry look like if you served non-customers?
- **Use the Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create Grid** in your next strategy offsite. Force every team to make explicit decisions, not just incremental improvements.
- **Identify your industry's alternative industries** — what are people using instead of your product? What can you learn from how alternatives solve the same problem?
- **Test the "headwind" in your blue ocean strategy**: What are the 20% of factors that will drive 80% of adoption? Focus there first.
- **Design for both functional *and* emotional appeal** — products that serve only one dimension are easier to replicate than those that create a new emotional category.
- **Build a blue ocean brand early** — when the rules are being written, brand becomes the moat. Be first and be known for defining the new category.

## Controversy / Criticism

- **The framework can be too abstract** — "create new market space" is easy to say, hard to do. The Six Paths help, but there are no guarantees.
- **Some critics argue it underweights competitive response** — in a world of fast followers and copycats, first-mover advantage in blue oceans may be short-lived.
- **The case studies** — while compelling — have been criticized for selection bias, focusing on successful examples and not analyzing failures of blue ocean attempts.
- **It can encourage over-investment in novel strategies** that are actually higher-risk than the framework implies.
- **The ERRC grid is a powerful tool but can become a checkbox exercise** if teams don't rigorously challenge their assumptions about what buyers actually value.

## One-Line Takeaway

> Stop trying to outcompete rivals in crowded markets — instead, reconstruct industry boundaries by eliminating what customers don't need, reducing what they settle for, raising what they desire, and creating what has never existed before.
