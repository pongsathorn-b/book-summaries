# Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman

## Overview

Nobel Prize-winning psychologist Daniel Kahneman reveals the two systems that drive how we think: System 1 (fast, intuitive, emotional) and System 2 (slow, deliberate, logical). Most of our decisions come from System 1 — and that's where the trouble starts.

## The Two Systems

### System 1: Fast Thinking
- Automatic, intuitive, effortless
- Makes snap judgments, sees patterns that aren't there
- Examples: reading a billboard, detecting hostility in a voice, driving on an empty road
- Prone to cognitive biases and logical errors

### System 2: Slow Thinking
- Deliberate, effortful, logical
- Activated when System 1 hits its limits
- Examples: counting, complex math, comparing products, filling out forms
- Lazy — System 2 often accepts System 1's answers without checking

## Key Biases

### Anchoring Effect
The first number you see anchors your judgment. Ask someone "Was Gandhi older or younger than 144 when he died?" and their subsequent age estimate will be higher than if you'd asked "older or younger than 35."

### Availability Heuristic
Things that are easier to recall seem more common. Plane crash coverage makes flying seem dangerous — despite being statistically the safest mode of transport.

### Loss Aversion
Losses loom larger than equivalent gains. Losing $100 feels worse than gaining $100 feels good. This is why "fear of loss" is a stronger motivator than "hope of gain."

### The Planning Fallacy
We consistently underestimate costs, risks, and time needed to complete projects — while overestimating our ability to execute. Look at actual similar projects, not optimistic plans.

### The Endowment Effect
We value what we own more than what we don't own. A $5 coffee mug given to you feels worth more when you try to sell it.

### Halo Effect
One positive trait bleeds into everything. An attractive person is judged more favorably on unrelated traits like intelligence or kindness.

## What "Thinking" Really Means

Most of our "thinking" is actually System 1 — a prediction machine, not a logic machine. System 2 is lazy and largely accepts System 1's output. We mistake fluency (ease of recall) for truth.

## For Founders

- First impressions of products, founders, and pitches are System 1 judgments — make the first 30 seconds count
- A/B tests reveal what System 2 actually prefers vs what people say they want (System 1 answers)
- Investor decisions are heavily influenced by anchoring and halo effects
- Forecasting requires actively engaging System 2 and challenging System 1's quick conclusions

## One-Line Takeaway

> Your intuition is System 1 talking — and it's often wrong about complex things. Slow down for decisions that matter.
