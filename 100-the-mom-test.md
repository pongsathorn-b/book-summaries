# The Mom Test — Rob Fitzpatrick

> "The Mom Test: You should ask people what they want, watch what they do, and find out what they actually pay for. The mom test is that you should never ask your mom whether your business idea is good — because she'll say yes."

## Overview

[[The Mom Test]] is a practical guide to customer discovery and product validation for entrepreneurs, named after a simple principle: your mother will tell you your idea is great even if it's terrible, so you need to ask questions in ways that reveal real behavior rather than polite opinions. Rob Fitzpatrick argues that most entrepreneurs fail not because they build bad products but because they spend years building products without ever properly validating whether anyone would actually buy them. The book is a ruthlessly practical guide to asking the right questions, interpreting the answers honestly, and using customer conversations to de-risk startup decisions before it's too late.

## Core Concepts

### Why the Mom Test Matters
Fitzpatrick's core insight is that people lie to entrepreneurs — not maliciously but because of social politeness and their own desire not to disappoint. When you ask "would you buy this?" people say yes because they want to be supportive. When you ask "is this a good idea?" everyone has an opinion and feels obligated to share it. The Mom Test gives you questions that are immune to this lying — questions that reveal actual behavior, actual priorities, and actual willingness to pay.

### Talking About Ideas vs. Talking About Behavior
The key distinction in customer discovery is between talking about hypothetical futures (would you buy this?) and actual past behavior (have you actually bought a similar product? have you actually tried to solve this problem?). Questions about past behavior are harder to lie about and more predictive of future buying decisions. "Have you bought a product like this before?" reveals more than "would you buy this?"

### The Three Rules of Customer Conversations
Fitzpatrick offers three rules for conversations that reveal truth:
1. Talk about their life, not your idea — focus the conversation on their actual experience, not your product concept
2. Ask about specifics in the past tense — not "would you" but "did you," not "do you want to" but "have you been looking for"
3. Talk less, listen more — the entrepreneur's job is to extract information, not deliver pitches; aim for 30% talking maximum

### Bad Customer Discovery: Pitching and Preaching
Most entrepreneurs make a fatal error in customer conversations: they pitch. They describe their product idea, explain its features, and ask for validation. This is counterproductive for two reasons: first, you anchor the conversation on your solution rather than their problem; second, you signal that you need validation, and social pressure produces false positives. The alternative is "learning conversations" focused on understanding the customer's world without presenting your solution.

### What You Should Be Learning
Effective customer discovery reveals five categories of information: the problem they actually experience, how they currently solve it (including products they've tried and abandoned), what they'd pay to solve it, who else is involved in the decision, and what the real purchase process looks like. Each of these requires different question framings and different attention to behavioral evidence.

### Identifying and Handling "Fluff"
Fluff is polite, meaningless conversation that provides no useful information — "this is a great idea," "I'd definitely use that," "this could be really big." Fitzpatrick provides a toolkit for recognizing fluff (usually vague, future-tense, hypothetical responses) and converting it to real information through follow-up questions that pin down specifics. "That's interesting, can you tell me about the last time that happened?" is the basic conversion tool.

### Pre-Qualifying Questions
Before committing to a full conversation, use pre-qualifying questions to screen for relevant respondents. "What's the biggest challenge you're facing with [topic]?" immediately separates people who actually have the problem from those who are just being polite. If they can't immediately name a specific challenge, they're not a useful conversation partner for validating your idea.

### Running the Process: How Many Conversations?
Fitzpatrick's rule of thumb: talk to at least 100 people who actually have the problem before concluding there's no market. But quality matters more than quantity — five well-conducted conversations that follow the Mom Test are worth more than 50 pitch-and-reassure conversations. The goal is to reach insight saturation, where you're hearing the same problems and behaviors repeatedly.

### Writing Results and Identifying Patterns
After each conversation, write down: what specific problem did they describe? What have they actually done about it? What did they show you (screenshots, receipts, competing products)? What would they actually pay for? These specific facts are the raw material for pattern recognition. When the same pattern appears across multiple conversations, you've found something real.

## Key Lessons

1. **Never ask "is this a good idea?"** — Ask instead about their actual past behavior, which they cannot lie about without being obvious
2. **Your mother will tell you it's great — that's the problem** — Polite positive feedback is not validation; learn to extract real behavioral evidence
3. **Talk about their life, not your idea** — The conversation should always return to their experience, not your solution
4. **Ask about specifics in the past tense** — "Did you actually buy something like this?" vs "Would you buy this?" — past tense questions are harder to fake
5. **The goal is 30% talking, 70% listening** — If you're talking more than listening, you're pitching, not learning
6. **Find the person who will actually pay, not just the user** — In B2B especially, the decision-maker and the user may be different people
7. **Watch for "fluff" and convert it to specifics** — Every vague positive statement should be followed with "tell me about the last time..."
8. **Pre-qualify ruthlessly** — Don't waste time on people who don't actually have the problem; find people who will pay for a real solution
9. **Quantity at quality's service** — Talk to 100+ people who actually have the problem; patterns only emerge from sufficient volume
10. **Document immediately after each conversation** — Write down specific facts while they're fresh; vague impressions are not useful data

## Practical Applications

- Before building anything, conduct at least 20 Mom Test conversations with people who match your target customer
- In every customer conversation, aim for 30% talking — if you hit 50%, you've probably been pitching
- Replace future-tense questions with past-tense questions in every conversation
- After every conversation, write down: specific problem described, what they've done about it, what they'd pay, who else is involved
- Pre-qualify by asking "what's your biggest challenge with X?" before investing time in full conversations
- When you hear fluff, follow up with "can you tell me about the last time that happened?" to convert to specifics
- Identify the purchase decision-maker, not just the user — who writes the check?
- Look for emotional language about real pain — "I hate this," "this costs me hours every week" is signal; "this could be useful" is noise
- Run cohort analysis on your conversation notes — after 20, 50, 100 conversations, what patterns emerge?
- Use customer conversations to kill features, not just validate them — ruthlessly cutting features based on evidence is more valuable than adding them

## Controversy/Criticism

- **Can produce analysis paralysis** — Some entrepreneurs use customer conversations as an excuse not to build anything, seeking certainty that will never come
- **Sample size and selection bias** — 100 conversations is still a small sample; people who agree to talk to entrepreneurs may not represent the broader market
- **Doesn't address product-market fit once you have initial traction** — The Mom Test is excellent for early validation but less useful once you have an existing product
- **Ignores the value of conviction** — Sometimes the most important insight is that the market doesn't yet know what it wants (Steve Jobs' position)
- **Customer conversations can lead you away from innovation** — Existing customers can only tell you about problems they know they have; transformative products often address unknown needs
- **Doesn't fully address the founder's role in driving direction** — Focusing too heavily on customer feedback can produce incremental improvements rather than breakthrough products

## One-Line Takeaway

The single biggest cause of startup failure is building something nobody actually wants — the Mom Test gives you questions immune to polite flattery so you learn what customers actually do, actually pay for, and actually need before you spend years building the wrong thing.
