# Finding Your True Ethics — Summary

> "The unexamined life is not worth living." — Socrates

## Overview

A guide to discovering your authentic ethical principles — not inherited from culture, religion, or family — but arrived at through rigorous self-examination. The goal is to live according to values you can rationally defend, not simply ones you were taught.

## Core Concepts

### The Difference Between Morality and Ethics

**Morality** = the received rules of your culture and upbringing
**Ethics** = the principles you arrive at through conscious reasoning

Most people live by inherited morality without ever examining whether those principles make sense for their life.

### Authenticity

Living authentically means your actions align with your examined values — not with external expectations. [[Authentic Living]] requires [[Values Clarification]].

### The Examining Mind

[[Socratic Method]] — the practice of asking "why do I believe this?" repeatedly until you reach bedrock beliefs that feel genuinely true.

### The Virtue of Honesty with Yourself

[[Self-Deception]] is the greatest ethical failure — not because it harms others directly, but because it makes all other failures possible.

## Key Lessons

1. **Inherited values are not your values** — unless you've examined them, they're just programming
2. **Question the "should"** — whenever you think "I should do X," ask why you believe that
3. **Values must be lived, not just held** — a belief is not real unless it shapes decisions under pressure
4. **Contradictions reveal growth edges** — if your values conflict, one of them isn't as solid as you thought
5. **Society's script is a default, not a choice** — you can write your own
6. **Ethics is ongoing** — values you examined at 20 may need re-examination at 40
7. **Courage is required** — examined values sometimes conflict with what others expect
8. **The examined life is harder but more fulfilling** — living by your own principles is harder than following the script
