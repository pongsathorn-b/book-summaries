# Welcome to Your Brain — Sandra Aamodt & Sam Wang

> "Your brain is more capable than myths suggest."

## Overview

Neuroscientists [[Sandra Aamodt]] and [[Sam Wang]] explain what neuroscience actually tells us — and debunk popular myths. Fascinating for anyone who's been told they only use 10% of their brain.

## Key Brain Facts

### You Don't Use Only 10% of Your Brain
False. Brain imaging shows we use all of it, just different parts at different times. [[Brain Myths]] are everywhere.

### [[Brain Plasticity]]

The brain rewires itself constantly based on experience. [[Learning Changes Brain Structure]]. This is why [[Expertise Exists]].

### [[Sleep and Memory]]

During sleep, the hippocampus replays the day's events for [[Long-Term Memory Storage]]. Without sufficient sleep, memories don't transfer.

### [[Stress Destroys Learning]]

[[Chronic Stress]] releases cortisol, which damages the hippocampus. [[Anxiety]] blocks [[Working Memory]].

## Debunking Popular Myths

### [[Brain Myths]]: "Follow Your Passion" Is Bad Advice
[[Passion Follows Competence]], not the other way around. Getting good at something and then loving it — that's more common.

### [[Brain Myths]]: Learning Styles Are Probably Wrong
Adapting teaching to "learning styles" (visual/auditory/kinesthetic) doesn't improve outcomes. Everyone learns better with multiple modalities.

### [[Brain Myths]]: Vitamins and "Brain Foods" Don't Work
The brain is already efficient. Supplements have minimal effect in people who are well-nourished. Real [[Brain Boosters]]: sleep, exercise, [[Stress Management]].

### Video Games Might Sharpen Attention
[[Action Video Games]] improve certain types of attention and spatial skills. The "video games rot your brain" claim doesn't hold up.

## Practical Takeaways

1. Sleep 7-9 hours — it's when real learning happens
2. Manage [[Exam Anxiety]] — [[Deep Breathing]] actually works
3. Exercise regularly — improves [[Cognitive Function]] and reduces [[Stress]]
4. Don't follow [[Brain Myths]] about passion — fall in love with [[Solving Problems]]
5. Use concrete, vivid examples when trying to [[Remember]]
