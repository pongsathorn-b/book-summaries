# Welcome to Your Brain — Sandra Aamodt & Sam Wang

## Overview

Neuroscientists Sandra Aamodt and Sam Wang explain what neuroscience actually tells us about how the brain works — and debunk popular myths. Written accessibly for non-scientists. Fascinating for anyone who's been told they only use 10% of their brain or that you should "follow your passion."

## Key Brain Facts

### You Don't Use Only 10% of Your Brain

False. Brain imaging shows we use all of it, just different parts at different times. The "10%" myth probably comes from misunderstanding that neurons are only 10% of brain cells — the other 90% are glial cells (support cells). You're using 100% of your brain.

### Your Brain Is Constantly Changing

The brain is plastic — it rewires itself constantly based on experience. Learning changes actual brain structure. This is why expertise actually exists — deliberate practice physically reshapes the brain.

###睡眠 Is Essential for Memory

Sleep is not rest — it's when the brain replays and consolidates what you've learned. Studies show: sleep deprivation impairs memory formation more than alcohol intoxication. 7-9 hours of sleep is when learning is stored.

### The Brain Prefers Specificity Over Abstraction

Abstract information is hard to learn. Concrete, vivid, emotionally charged information is remembered better. This is why storytelling works — concrete scenes stick.

### Stress Destroys Learning

Chronic stress releases cortisol, which damages the hippocampus (memory center). The anxiety-performance loop: students who are too anxious underperform despite knowing the material. Managing stress is part of studying.

### The Importance of Sleep for Memory (Repeated for Emphasis)

Aamodt and Wang are very clear: sleep is not optional for learning. During sleep, the hippocampus replays the day's events to the cortex for long-term storage. Without sufficient sleep, memories don't transfer and are essentially lost.

## Debunking Popular Myths

### "Follow Your Passion" Is Bad Advice

Passion tends to follow competence, not precede it. You don't start out passionate about something you can't do yet. Getting good at something and then loving it — that's more common. Don't drop everything because you "don't feel passion" for your current path.

### Learning Styles (Visual/Auditory/Kinesthetic) Are Probably Wrong

Studies show that adapting teaching to "learning styles" doesn't improve outcomes. Everyone learns better with multiple modalities. The brain doesn't have separate channels for different content types.

### Vitamins and "Brain Foods" Don't Work

The brain is already efficient. Supplements, "brain foods," and most nootropics have minimal effect in people who are already well-nourished. The real brain boosters: sleep, exercise, and managing stress.

### Video Games Might Actually Sharpen Attention

Contrary to the moral panic: studies suggest action video games improve certain types of attention and spatial skills. Not a reason to game all day — but the "video games rot your brain" claim doesn't hold up.

## Practical Takeaways

1. Sleep 7-9 hours — it's when real learning happens
2. Manage exam/test anxiety — techniques like deep breathing actually work
3. Exercise regularly — improves cognitive function and reduces stress
4. Don't follow passion blindly — competence often precedes passion
5. Use concrete, vivid examples when trying to remember or understand something

## For Founders

- Understanding the brain's limitations: decision fatigue is real (SOBs)
- The "passion" myth is dangerous for startup decisions — fall in love with solving the problem, not the initial excitement
- Sleep before important presentations and pitches

## One-Line Takeaway

> Your brain is more plastic and capable than myths suggest — but it needs sleep, low stress, and deliberate practice to perform at its best.
