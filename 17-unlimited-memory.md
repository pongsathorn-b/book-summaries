# 17 — Unlimited Memory: How to Use Advanced Learning Strategies to Become a Master Learner

**By Kevin Horsley (2014)**

---

## Overview

*Unlimited Memory* is a practical guide to advanced memory techniques by Kevin Horsley, a Canadian memory expert and former World Memory Championship competitor who has coached individuals and organizations on memory improvement for over two decades. Horsley approaches memory improvement not as a party trick or curiosity, but as a **core life skill** that can be systematically developed through the mastery of specific mental strategies. The book is organized around the major memory techniques — the memory palace (method of loci), chunking, association, visualization, and the phonetic number system — and shows how to apply each to real-world learning tasks: remembering names, speeches, foreign language vocabulary, books, numbers, and complex professional information. Horsley writes in a direct, no-fluff style, sharing personal anecdotes from his memory competition experience while grounding every technique in the cognitive science that explains why it works.

## Core Concepts

### The Memory Palace (Method of Loci) — The Master Technique
Horsley treats the memory palace as the foundation of all advanced memory work. The principle is simple: your brain has exceptional spatial and navigational memory — you can recall the layout of your home, the route to school, or a favorite hiking trail in extraordinary detail without effort. The memory palace exploits this by converting abstract information into vivid, unusual, spatially located mental images and placing them along a familiar route or within a familiar building. To recall, you mentally walk the route and see the images in their locations. The technique has been used by memory champions, ancient orators, and medieval scholars for millennia. Horsley teaches it systematically: selecting and preparing a route, placing images, and reviewing at spaced intervals.

### Association: Connecting New Information to Existing Knowledge
Memory is associative — new information is most easily stored when it connects to something you already know well. Horsley uses this principle to anchor new memories to established knowledge through deliberate, vivid links. When learning a new concept, ask: *"What does this remind me of? What can I connect this to?"* The more unexpected and vivid the connection, the more memorable. Association works hand-in-hand with the memory palace: the images you place in a palace should themselves be rich with associations.

### Visualization and the "Weirder the Better" Principle
Horsley advocates for deliberately vivid, unusual, often bizarre and exaggerated mental imagery. The brain's novelty-detection systems flag unexpected, emotionally charged, or physically exaggerated images as important — these receive priority encoding and retrieval. A standard image of "apple" is forgettable; "a burning apple orbiting a purple moon while a giant eats it" is memorable. Horsley pushes this principle aggressively, recommending that practitioners make their images as gross, sexual, surprising, or physically extreme as needed to create an indelible impression.

### Chunking: Organizing Information into Meaningful Groups
Working memory can hold approximately 7 (plus or minus 2) items at once. Chunking organizes scattered information into meaningful groups that fit within this limit. A phone number broken into 3 chunks (514-882-9031) is far easier to remember than 10 separate digits. Horsley extends this principle beyond numbers — apply it to names, ideas, lists, and sequences of any kind. Good chunking requires finding the meaningful structure within the information, not just arbitrarily grouping.

### The Phonetic Number System (Major System)
For memorizing numbers — phone numbers, dates, formulas, sequences — Horsley teaches the phonetic (Major) system, in which each digit is assigned a consonant sound. Numbers are converted to words by filling in vowels, creating vivid images. This allows sequences of digits to be encoded as memorable, visual, palace-placed images. With practice, this system allows memorization of hundreds of digits, complete phone directories, and complex numerical data.

### The PAO System (Person-Action-Object)
Building on the phonetic system, Horsley introduces the **PAO** method: for each two-digit number (00–99), you pre-create a unique Person performing a unique Action on a unique Object. Numbers are then converted to sequences of PAO triples, creating vivid, interactive scenes that are easy to place in a palace and nearly impossible to forget. This system is a staple of memory competition champions.

### Spaced Repetition: The Forgetting Curve in Your Favor
Horsley emphasizes that even the best memory techniques require reinforcement. Memory traces weaken without review, and each review strengthens the trace if spaced appropriately. He recommends reviewing material placed in a memory palace after 1 day, then 3 days, then 1 week, then 2 weeks, and so on — gradually extending intervals. This approach aligns with Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve and can produce near-permanent retention with minimal review.

### Concentration and Focus as Prerequisites
A scattered, distracted mind cannot effectively encode or retrieve memories. Horsley insists that memory improvement begins with **focus** — the ability to concentrate fully on the material at hand during encoding. Without genuine attention, even the best technique fails. He treats attention training as a foundational practice, separate from but supportive of the mnemonic techniques.

### Reading a Book a Day — The Horsley Method
Horsley is known for his "book a day" reading habit, enabled by his memory techniques. His approach to reading for retention involves: a quick preview (headings, structure), active reading with a questioning posture, taking structured notes using memory palace techniques, and reviewing using the spaced repetition schedule. The key is that he doesn't just read — he encodes and files what he reads so it can be retrieved and used.

## Key Lessons

1. **The memory palace is your primary tool.** Master it, and every other technique builds on it. Invest the time to learn it properly — it will serve you for life.
2. **Association is the bridge.** New memories stick when they connect to existing knowledge. Always ask: *"What does this link to in my mind?"*
3. **Vivid, unusual imagery is essential.** The weirder, more exaggerated, more physically surprising the image, the more indelible the memory. Do not be timid.
4. **Chunking is organizational intelligence.** Find the meaningful structure in information and use it. What looks like complexity often has a simple organizing principle.
5. **Numbers are learnable.** With the phonetic system and PAO, any sequence of numbers can be transformed into memorable visual scenes.
6. **Review with increasing intervals.** Spaced repetition is not optional — it is the mechanism by which short-term memory becomes permanent.
7. **Focus is the entry price.** You cannot memorize what you are not genuinely paying attention to. Train your concentration before you train your technique.
8. **Memory is a skill, not a talent.** Kevin Horsley was not born with exceptional memory — he built it. You can too.

## Practical Applications

- **Remembering names and faces**: Associate each person's name with a vivid image related to their face (the first thing their name reminds you of), then place that image on them in your mind.
- **Learning foreign language vocabulary**: Create vivid scenes in a memory palace that link the foreign word to its meaning through phonetic association or visual pun.
- **Memorizing speeches**: Break the speech into major sections, assign each a location in your palace, and place vivid, sequential images representing the key ideas or words. Walk the palace when presenting.
- **Retaining books and articles**: After reading, walk through your palace placing images representing each key concept. Review the palace at increasing intervals over the following weeks.
- **Memorizing numbers (formulas, dates, sequences)**: Convert numbers to images using the phonetic system or PAO. Place these images in your palace for structured recall.
- **For professional learning**: Use chunking and association to connect new professional knowledge to what you already know. Place the connections in your palace for structured retrieval.
- **Building a daily review habit**: Spend 10 minutes each evening walking through your active palaces — the material you're currently encoding. This 10 minutes replaces hours of passive rereading.

## One-Line Takeaway

> Kevin Horsley demonstrates that photographic memory is a skill built through disciplined use of the memory palace, association, visualization, and spaced repetition — and that anyone can learn to remember anything by encoding it in the right way.
